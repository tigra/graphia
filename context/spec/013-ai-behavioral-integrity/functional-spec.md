# Functional Specification: AI Behavioral Integrity & Outcome Tracking

- **Roadmap Item:** Not a roadmap feature — an evidence-driven quality increment in the spirit of the Day-phase integrity trio (007–009) and the blunder-tracking work (011). Triggered directly by spec 011's first reliable (n=20) baseline, which exposed concrete behavioral pathologies. (Roadmap order unaffected; Phase 5 — Configurable Role Counts / Multi-Round Mafia Consensus — remains the next *feature*.)
- **Status:** Draft
- **Author:** Alexey Tigarev

---

## 1. Overview and Rationale (The "Why")

Spec 011's first well-powered measurement (20 games per provider, with confidence intervals) turned three AI behaviours from anecdote into evidence — and they badly break the social-deduction illusion:

- An AI **votes to execute *itself*** most of the time it's the target (measured 0.63 on the local model). A player trying to kill themselves is nonsensical.
- A Mafia AI **votes to execute its own teammate** almost always (measured 0.86), and **starts** such votes half the time — the Mafia don't act like a team at all.
- On the cloud model, AI players **never call a vote to execute anyone** across 20 whole games — the Day phase becomes a silent pass-through to Night, with every elimination coming from night-kills. The game's central drama (accuse, argue, vote) simply doesn't happen.

All three share one root cause: when an AI speaks or casts a ballot, **it is never told its own secret role, which side it's on, or who its teammates are.** It reasons like a generic bystander about names on a list — so it votes against itself, against its own team, or never accuses anyone.

This increment **first extends the measurement** — tracking, per recorded run, which side won and how much vote activity each side generated by game-day — **captures a baseline of those new measurements on the current behaviour**, and only **then fixes the behaviour** so AI players act consistently with their hidden role and actually participate in the Day. That ordering is deliberate: the new metrics exist precisely to *prove the fix worked*, so they must be in place and baselined against the unchanged behaviour before any prompt changes — the same measure-first discipline that, at spec 011, reversed a misleading small-sample conclusion.

**Desired outcome:** AI players stop committing self- and team-defeating votes, the Day phase comes alive with genuine accusations and executions, and the maintainer can see — in the quality ledger — both the reduced blunder rates *and* their effect on who wins and how the game actually plays out.

**Success is measured by:** after the change, a measured batch of games shows the self-execution and teammate-execution vote rates dropping sharply (confidence intervals clearly below the recorded baseline), AI players initiating votes on both providers (the Day phase no longer silent), and the ledger carrying — for every run — the win-rate by side and the vote-initiation activity broken down by side and by game-day.

## 2. Functional Requirements (The "What")

### 2.1 The quality ledger tracks game outcomes (win-rate by side)

- **As the** maintainer, **I want** each recorded run to capture which side won and how often, **so that** I can see whether the AI's behaviour actually affects who wins — not just whether individual blunders went down.
  - **Acceptance Criteria:**
    - [ ] Each recorded run carries **how many games each side won** (and any that ended without a win, e.g. hit the round cap), shown as a **win-rate per side** alongside the existing behaviour rates.
    - [ ] The record notes the **caveat** that these games are played with the evaluation's standard scripted human (who plays passively), so the win-rate is a **consistent, comparable** measure across runs — not a claim about true game balance.

### 2.2 The quality ledger tracks vote-initiation activity by side and by day

- **As the** maintainer, **I want** each run to show how much vote activity each side generated and when, **so that** a silent Day phase (like the cloud model's zero votes) is a visible number rather than something I have to infer from missing data.
  - **Acceptance Criteria:**
    - [ ] Each recorded run carries the **count of vote-to-execute initiations broken down by the initiating side** (Mafia vs Law-abiding) **and by game-day** (Day 1, Day 2, …).
    - [ ] A run in which **no votes were initiated** shows that as an explicit zero-activity reading, so "the Day phase did nothing" is unmistakable at a glance (not an empty/absent value to be guessed at).

### 2.3 The new facts appear in the ledger viewer

- **As the** maintainer, **I want** the new outcome and vote-activity facts to be visible in the ledger viewer, **so that** I can read them the same way I read the existing metrics.
  - **Acceptance Criteria:**
    - [ ] The win-rate-by-side and the vote-activity breakdown are **visible for each run in the viewer** — at least in the full-record drill-down, and surfaced in the table where they fit.

### 2.4 The improvement is demonstrated against a before-picture

- **As the** maintainer, **I want** the new measurements captured on the current behaviour before the fixes are applied, **so that** the fixes' effect is provable, not asserted.
  - **Acceptance Criteria:**
    - [ ] A **baseline** recording of the new outcome and vote-activity metrics is taken on the **unchanged** AI behaviour and committed, **before** the behaviour fixes are applied — so the after-fix run can be compared against a real before-picture in the same ledger.

### 2.5 AI players act consistently with their own role and team

- **As a** player, **I want** the AI players to behave as if they know who they are and whose side they're on, **so that** the game feels like real social deduction rather than confused bystanders.
  - **Acceptance Criteria:**
    - [ ] **The grounding gives each AI only the knowledge its role legitimately has.** A Mafioso is told its own role, its goal, and **its fellow Mafiosi**; a Law-abiding Citizen is told only its own role and goal — **never who any other player is**, and in particular **never who the other Law-abiding Citizens are**. The deduction game depends on the townsfolk *not* knowing one another; nothing in this change may disclose a player's allegiance to someone whose role wouldn't already know it.
    - [ ] An AI **rarely votes to execute itself** — sparing itself becomes the strong default, not the rarity. (Measured: the self-approval rate drops sharply, confidence interval clearly below the recorded baseline of ~0.63. A **rare strategic self-sacrifice** is tolerated — a player who's already doomed, or building credibility with the town, may choose not to fight their own execution — so this is a strong reduction by persuasion, not an absolute ban. *Starting* a vote against oneself remains structurally prevented, as today.)
    - [ ] A Mafia AI **does not help execute a fellow Mafia member** — it does not start a vote against a teammate, and votes to spare rather than execute one. (Measured: the teammate-execution rates drop sharply, confidence intervals clearly below the recorded baselines of ~0.50 initiation and ~0.86 approval. A rare strategic "bussing" exception is tolerated; the point is that protecting teammates becomes the strong default, not the rarity.)
    - [ ] The change is verified by **measurement across a batch of real games** (against the §2.4 before-picture), not a single hand-played game, consistent with how the project evaluates non-deterministic AI behaviour.

### 2.6 AI players participate in the Day phase

- **As a** player, **I want** the AI to raise suspicions and call votes during the Day when warranted, **so that** the Day phase is a real debate with consequences, not a silent wait for Night.
  - **Acceptance Criteria:**
    - [ ] Across a measured batch of games, **AI players initiate vote-to-execute calls on both providers** — the Day phase produces genuine accusations and at least some day-executions, rather than every elimination coming from night-kills. (Visible in the §2.2 vote-activity tracking: the after-fix run shows non-zero initiations on both providers where the baseline showed zero on the cloud model.)
    - [ ] AI players still **don't accuse recklessly** — they speak most turns and reserve a vote for a concrete suspicion, so the Day is lively but not a vote on every turn.

## 3. Scope and Boundaries

### In-Scope

- **Fixing AI behaviour** so players act consistently with their hidden role and team (no self-execution votes; Mafia protect teammates) and **participate in the Day** (accuse / call votes when warranted) — on **both** providers.
- **Extending the quality measurement** to record game **outcomes (win-rate by side)** and **vote-initiation activity by side and by game-day**, and showing these in the **ledger viewer**.
- Capturing a **before-fix baseline** of the new metrics so the improvement is demonstrable (measure-first within the spec).
- A documented **caveat** that win-rate is measured under the evaluation's passive scripted human — comparable across runs, not a true-balance claim.

### Out-of-Scope

- **Changing how the human plays in the evaluation** (the scripted human stays passive) — keeping it constant preserves comparability with the existing n=20 baseline; a more active eval-human is a separate concern.
- **Re-balancing the game** (role counts, win conditions, Mafia size) — Phase 5 territory; this spec changes AI *behaviour quality*, not the rules.
- **New blunder categories** beyond those already tracked in spec 011 — this spec fixes the known ones and adds outcome/activity tracking, not new detectors.
- **A before/after comparison tool or charts in the viewer** — the viewer shows the numbers (and the new facts); comparison stays by eye, per spec 012's boundary.
- **All roadmap items** (Phase 5 Configurable Role Counts & Multi-Round Mafia Consensus; Phase 6 Personas & Async Day Chat; Phase 7 Tool-Use & Expanded Roles) — each its own spec.
